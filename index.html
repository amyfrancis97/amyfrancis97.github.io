A perspective on AI in science

AI is going to be part of everyday science.
It should work for everyone.

Science is changing fast, and AI is a big part of why. The question worth asking is not what these tools can do in theory - it's who actually gets to use them.

"Some of the most powerful tools in the history of biology are sitting in research papers and GitHub repos that most bench scientists have never heard of. That feels like a problem worth working on."

The past few years have produced some remarkable models. AlphaFold more or less solved protein structure prediction, a problem that had been open for 50 years. ESM-2 showed that a language model trained on protein sequences alone could pick up on evolutionary relationships that took decades of wet-lab work to characterise. These are not small steps forward - they represent a real change in what is possible.

But using these models well still requires a fairly unusual mix of skills - enough machine learning to run and adapt them, enough compute to actually train or fine-tune them, and enough domain knowledge to know what to do with the output. Most biologists have one of those things, maybe two. The gap between what these tools can do and what most researchers can access is real, and it matters.

Something is also shifting in how the industry is organised. Pharmaceutical companies are increasingly partnering with smaller technology companies - Cradle, Twist Bioscience, Recursion, Insilico Medicine - rather than trying to build everything in-house. That collaboration between deep biological knowledge and modern ML tooling is starting to compress drug discovery timelines in ways that would have seemed unlikely five years ago. Whether that means genuinely better drugs, fewer side effects, and access to molecular spaces that were previously out of reach is a question I think about a lot. I write about it in the blog.

I came into data science from the biology side, so I know what it feels like to have a question you cannot answer because the tools are out of reach. That is what drives most of what I build - whether that is an RNA-seq pipeline with a sensible interface, a flow cytometry tool that a non-programmer can actually run, or an event that gets ML researchers and biologists in the same room. I want AI to be something scientists just use, the way they use R or a pipette.

Some foundation models worth knowing about

AlphaFold 2 & 3
DeepMind / Google
Predicted the 3D structure of virtually every known protein. AlphaFold 3 extends this to DNA, RNA, and small molecules, which opens up a lot of new possibilities for drug design.
ESM-2 & ESMFold
Meta AI
Protein language models trained on 250M sequences. Encode evolutionary relationships and enable zero-shot fitness and function prediction - the models I've used directly in my own research.
Evo
Arc Institute
A genomic foundation model trained across the tree of life - 7B parameters on DNA - enabling generative design of genes, regulatory elements, and CRISPR guides.
RFdiffusion
Baker Lab, UW
Generative diffusion model for de novo protein design. Can hallucinate functional proteins - binders, enzymes, vaccine candidates - from scratch, with experimental validation.
Geneformer
Broad Institute
A transformer pre-trained on 30M single-cell transcriptomes. Enables in silico gene perturbation and network inference without costly wet-lab experiments.
scGPT
University of Toronto
Foundation model for single-cell biology - pre-trained on 33M cells across tissues. Enables cell type annotation, perturbation response prediction, and GRN inference.
GeminiMol / MolBERT
Various
Molecular foundation models trained on SMILES and 3D conformers, useful for property prediction, virtual screening, and de novo drug design.
TxGNN
Harvard / Broad
Graph neural network trained on biomedical knowledge graphs to predict drug indications and contraindications - useful for identifying new uses for existing drugs.
BioNeMo
NVIDIA
A platform for training and deploying biological foundation models - wrapping models like ESMFold, MolMIM, and DiffDock behind accessible APIs so researchers can use them without managing the underlying infrastructure themselves.
DiffDock & MolMIM
NVIDIA / MIT
DiffDock uses diffusion to predict how a drug molecule binds to a protein target - much faster than traditional docking methods. MolMIM generates novel molecules optimised for specific properties.

Getting these models into the hands of researchers who know what to do with the biology is a different challenge to building them. There is also a broader shift happening in how pharma companies are approaching this - worth a longer look in the blog.
